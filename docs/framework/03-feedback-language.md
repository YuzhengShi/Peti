# Feedback Language Rules

> Source: Framework Section 12

## Feedback Template Structure

For EVERY domain, your interpretation MUST follow this 5-part structure:

### 1. Domain Introduction
Brief explanation of what the construct means in everyday terms. Not clinical. Not jargon.

Example (Emotion Regulation):
"Emotion regulation describes the patterns you use to notice, understand, and respond to your emotions. Everyone regulates emotions differently, and patterns can vary depending on stress, context, and what you've learned works for you."

### 2. Pattern Description
A narrative paragraph describing the user's results using their specific response pattern, written in second person ("you").

Example (Higher Emotion Regulation):
"Your responses suggest that you generally have effective ways of managing emotions when they arise. You tend to notice your emotions, understand what's driving them, and have strategies for responding that work for you. This doesn't mean emotions are always easy — everyone struggles sometimes — but you seem to have developed patterns that help you navigate emotional experiences."

Example (Lower Emotion Regulation):
"Your responses suggest that managing emotions has been challenging recently. You may find it difficult to step back when emotions feel intense, or you might not always have strategies that help you feel more settled. This is a common experience, especially during stressful periods, and emotion regulation skills can be developed and strengthened over time."

### 3. What This Means in Daily Life
Concrete examples of how this pattern might show up in everyday situations. Avoid overgeneralization.

Example:
"In daily life, this might mean you're able to calm yourself down after a frustrating situation, or you can talk through difficult feelings with others without becoming overwhelmed. It might also mean you can recognize when you need space or support."

### 4. Context and Variability
Acknowledgment that patterns vary by situation and are influenced by current circumstances.

Example:
"Keep in mind that emotion regulation can vary depending on what's happening in your life. Stress, sleep, relationships, and major life changes all affect how easily you can manage emotions. Your current pattern reflects recent experiences but isn't necessarily permanent."

### 5. Connection to Other Domains (When Relevant)
Transparent explanation of expected overlap with other assessed domains.

Example:
"Emotion regulation often relates to sleep quality and daily functioning. If you're experiencing difficulties in multiple areas, they may be influencing each other rather than representing separate problems."

---

## Normalization of Variation

ALL feedback must explicitly normalize variation. Use language like:

- "Everyone varies in this area, and there's no single 'right' level."
- "This pattern is common and doesn't indicate a problem or disorder."
- "Many people experience similar patterns, especially during [relevant life stage/context]."
- "Variation in [domain] is part of normal human diversity."

---

## Modifiability Gradients

Feedback MUST differentiate between more stable and more modifiable patterns. Use these framings:

| Domain | Modifiability Language |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Personality traits** | "Traits are relatively stable but can and do change across adulthood, especially in response to new experiences and intentional effort." |
| **Personality functioning** | "These patterns can develop and strengthen over time with self-reflection, new relationship experiences, and sometimes therapy or coaching." |
| **Emotion regulation** | "Emotion regulation skills can be learned and practiced. Many people find these patterns shift with stress management, therapy, or intentional skill-building." |
| **Sleep** | "Sleep patterns often improve with changes to sleep habits, stress management, and addressing underlying factors affecting rest." |
| **Attachment** | "Relationship patterns can shift with new relationship experiences, self-awareness, and sometimes therapy focused on attachment." |
| **Daily functioning** | "Daily functioning often improves when underlying factors (sleep, stress, health, support) are addressed." |

---

## Tentative Language (MANDATORY)

NEVER write with certainty about what a person is. Always use:

- "Your responses suggest..." NOT "You are..."
- "You tend to..." NOT "You always..."
- "This pattern reflects..." NOT "This means you..."
- "You may find..." NOT "You will..."
- "It seems like..." NOT "It is clear that..."

Also include:
- "This assessment provides a snapshot based on your recent experiences and self-perceptions."
- "Consider whether these results match your own sense of yourself."

---

## Strength-Based and Balanced Interpretation

- Highlight patterns as **descriptive rather than deficient** — all trait levels and patterns are variations, not problems
- **Contextualize results** — patterns reflect interactions between person and environment, not fixed internal deficits
- **Emphasize modifiability** — note which domains are more changeable
- **Avoid pathologizing language** — use neutral, descriptive terms, never clinical or deficit-focused terminology
- **Lead with the positive** — if you must note a challenge, pair it with something affirming first
- **8th-10th grade reading level** — plain language, no jargon, define any psychological terms used

---

## What NOT to Do

- Never use disorder labels, cutoffs, or clinical categories
- Never suggest diagnosis or treatment
- Never share numeric scores, percentiles, or band thresholds
- Never write clinical report language ("the patient presents with...", "assessment reveals...")
- Never use corporate wellness jargon ("practice self-care", "set healthy boundaries")
- Never list things mechanically ("Here are 3 ways to...")
- Never compare the user to others ("most people score higher in...")
