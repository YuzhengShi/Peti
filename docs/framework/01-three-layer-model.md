# Three-Layer Conceptual Model

> Source: Framework Sections 1-2 (Executive Overview + Conceptual Framework)

## The Hierarchy

This assessment framework is organized as a three-layer hierarchy. When interpreting results, always keep this structure in mind — it determines how stable or changeable each domain is, and how you frame it to the user.

### Layer 1: Personality Traits (Most Stable)

**Domain**: Big Five (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism)

- Broad, relatively stable patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving
- Describe typical tendencies across situations and over time
- Show moderate consistency but CAN and DO change across adulthood, especially in response to new experiences, relationships, and life transitions
- Assessed as general tendencies (no specific timeframe)
- Frame as: "descriptive tendencies, not identities"

**Key evidence**: Bleidorn et al.'s meta-analysis (N=178,503) found rank-order stability plateaus in young adulthood, but Specht et al. (N=14,718) showed "personality changes throughout the life span, partly attributable to social demands and experiences."

### Layer 2: Modifiable Regulatory Systems and Patterns

**Domains**: Personality Functioning, Emotion Regulation, Attachment Patterns, Sleep-Wake Regulation

- Operate at a different timescale and level of analysis than traits
- Represent processes, capacities, and regulatory mechanisms
- Show GREATER modifiability in response to intervention, life circumstances, and developmental change
- Each has its own timeframe (emotion regulation: 2 weeks; sleep: 7 days; attachment & functioning: general patterns)
- Frame as: "patterns that can develop and change"

**Key evidence**: Stanton et al. found emotion regulation has "incremental predictive power beyond personality traits." Nielsen et al. found attachment-anxiety association "was mediated by emotion dysregulation" — these systems interact.

### Layer 3: Daily Functioning (Outcome Anchor)

**Domain**: Daily Functioning (WHODAS-based)

- Real-world activity and participation across life domains
- Conceptually distinct from both traits and symptoms
- Influenced by but not reducible to personality characteristics
- Most responsive to change — improves when underlying factors (sleep, stress, health, support) are addressed
- Assessed over past 30 days
- Frame as: "how life has been going recently, not who you are"

**Key evidence**: ICF framework emphasizes "function, not the health condition" and "categorizes the situation, not the person."

## Why This Matters for Profile Generation

When writing the profile:
1. **Traits** get framed as stable tendencies with room for growth: "You tend to..."
2. **Regulatory systems** get framed as current patterns that can shift: "Recently, you've been..."
3. **Functioning** gets framed as a snapshot: "Over the past month, things have been..."

Never treat any domain as permanent or fixed. But acknowledge that traits change more slowly than regulatory patterns, and regulatory patterns change more slowly than functioning.

## Cross-Layer Connections

The framework explicitly acknowledges bidirectional influence between layers:
- Traits influence regulatory systems (e.g., higher neuroticism → more difficulty with emotion regulation)
- Regulatory systems influence functioning (e.g., poor sleep → reduced daily functioning)
- Functioning feeds back to regulatory systems (e.g., social isolation → shifts in attachment patterns)

When you see consistent patterns across layers, name the connection. Example: "Your responses suggest a pattern where sleep difficulties may be affecting how you manage emotions day-to-day, which in turn shows up in some areas of daily functioning."
