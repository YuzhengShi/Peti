# Domain: Relationship / Attachment Patterns

> Source: Framework Section 8
> Layer: 2 (Modifiable Regulatory System)
> Timeframe: General relationship patterns (no specific timeframe)
> Items: 12 (ECR-R conceptual framework)
> Response scale: 7-point (Strongly disagree → Strongly agree)

## What This Domain Measures

Adult attachment describes individual differences in how people experience closeness, distance, and security in close relationships. These patterns are rooted in early caregiving experiences but continue to develop across the lifespan.

Conceptualized as **dimensional relationship patterns rather than fixed attachment "styles" or categories**. Fraley et al.'s taxometric analysis (N=21,186) confirmed "individual differences appear more consistent with a dimensional rather than a categorical model."

The framework deliberately avoids pathologizing labels like "fearful-avoidant" or "preoccupied" while capturing meaningful individual differences.

## Subscales (2 Independent Dimensions)

| Subscale | Items | What It Captures |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **anxiety** | 1-6 | Sensitivity to distance/rejection: worry about abandonment, need for reassurance, fear that others don't care as much, anxiety when someone important seems distant, fear of being truly known, worry about relationships ending |
| **avoidance** | 7-12 | Discomfort with closeness/dependency: preference for hiding feelings, discomfort with emotional closeness, difficulty depending on others, preference for emotional distance, difficulty opening up, discomfort with being very close (item 12 reverse-scored) |

**Critical**: These two dimensions are **independent, not opposite ends of one scale**. A person can be high on both (high anxiety + high avoidance), low on both, or any combination.

## How to Interpret Each Band

### Anxiety Dimension

**Lower anxiety**:
"Your responses suggest relatively low sensitivity to rejection or abandonment in close relationships. You tend not to worry excessively about whether people care about you or whether relationships will last. This pattern often reflects a secure foundation in how you approach closeness."

**Moderate anxiety**:
"Your responses suggest some sensitivity to distance in close relationships — you may sometimes worry about whether people care about you or notice when someone seems to pull away. This is a common and normal pattern that many people experience to some degree."

**Higher anxiety**:
"Your responses suggest heightened sensitivity to signs of rejection or distance in close relationships. You may worry more than you'd like about whether people truly care, or feel anxious when someone important seems distant. This is an understandable pattern — often rooted in experiences where closeness felt uncertain — and it can shift with new relationship experiences and self-awareness."

### Avoidance Dimension

**Lower avoidance**:
"Your responses suggest you're generally comfortable with emotional closeness and depending on others. You tend to open up relatively easily and don't feel a strong need to maintain emotional distance."

**Moderate avoidance**:
"Your responses suggest a mixed comfort level with closeness — sometimes you're comfortable letting people in, other times you may hold back or prefer some emotional distance. This often depends on the specific relationship and context."

**Higher avoidance**:
"Your responses suggest you tend to prefer emotional distance in close relationships. You may find it difficult to open up, depend on others, or let people get too close emotionally. This is a common pattern — often a protective response learned over time — and it doesn't mean you don't value connection. Relationship patterns can shift with new experiences and awareness."

## Derived Attachment Pattern (For Internal Use)

Based on the combination of anxiety and avoidance bands, you can identify a general pattern — but **never label it directly to the user**. Use it to inform the narrative tone:

| Anxiety | Avoidance | Internal Pattern | Narrative Approach |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Lower | Lower | Secure-leaning | Affirm their comfort with closeness |
| Higher | Lower | Anxious-leaning | Acknowledge sensitivity to distance; affirm desire for connection |
| Lower | Higher | Avoidant-leaning | Acknowledge comfort with independence; note that connection matters differently |
| Higher | Higher | Disorganized-leaning | Acknowledge the tension between wanting closeness and finding it difficult; normalize |

## Interpretation Framework Rules

1. **Relationship patterns, not attachment styles**: NEVER use labels like "anxious," "avoidant," "secure," or "fearful" in the profile
2. **Dimensional and independent**: Emphasize that the two dimensions are separate
3. **Context-dependent**: Patterns may vary across different relationships (romantic, friends, family)
4. **Developmentally influenced but modifiable**: Acknowledge early origins while emphasizing that patterns can change
5. **Non-pathologizing**: Frame all patterns as understandable ways of navigating closeness, not deficits or disorders

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **Attachment → Emotion regulation**: "The significant association between anxiety and attachment anxiety was mediated by emotion dysregulation" (Nielsen et al.). Higher attachment anxiety often co-occurs with more emotional difficulty.
- **Attachment ↔ Personality functioning**: Both involve how you experience yourself in relationships. Personality functioning = overall coherence; attachment = specific closeness/distance tendencies.
- **Neuroticism → Attachment anxiety**: Richter et al. found attachment dimensions "partly mediated the effect of neuroticism." Higher neuroticism often co-occurs with higher attachment anxiety.
- **Agreeableness → Attachment**: Attachment dimensions "fully mediated the effect of agreeableness on romantic jealousy."
- **Attachment → Daily functioning (getting-along)**: Attachment patterns often show up in the getting-along subscale of daily functioning.

## Modifiability

**Moderately modifiable.** "Relationship patterns can shift with new relationship experiences, self-awareness, and sometimes therapy focused on attachment." More stable than sleep or emotion regulation, but clearly not fixed — they developed through relationship experiences and can change through new ones.
