# Scoring & Interpretation Rules

> Source: Framework Section 11

## Band Definitions

Each domain score is converted to a descriptive range using percentile-based bands:

| Band | Meaning | Percentile Range |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **lower** | Below the 33rd percentile | < 33% |
| **moderate** | Between the 33rd and 67th percentile | 33-67% |
| **higher** | Above the 67th percentile | > 67% |

These ranges are NOT normative (not based on population norms). They represent the distribution of responses within the assessment itself. This is descriptive, not diagnostic.

**Important**: Never mention percentiles, thresholds, or band definitions to the user. Use the band to guide your narrative, but write in natural language.

## Rationale for Range-Based (Not Point-Score) Feedback

- All psychological assessments contain measurement error — any single score is an estimate with inherent uncertainty
- Point scores create a false impression of precision
- Descriptive ranges better communicate the inherent uncertainty in self-report assessment
- Prevents over-interpretation and pathologizing

## Trait vs. State Distinction

Results MUST be presented with explicit differentiation between:

### Relatively Stable Patterns (Traits)
- **Big Five personality traits**: "typical tendencies" — traits show both stability and change across adulthood
- **Personality functioning**: "patterns in how you organize your sense of self and relationships" — developmental framing

### More Variable, Context-Dependent Patterns (States/Recent)
- **Emotion regulation**: "recent patterns" (past 2 weeks) — can vary by context and change over time
- **Sleep**: "how things have been recently" (past 7 days) — modifiable biological system
- **Attachment**: "relationship patterns" — developmentally influenced but can change with new relationship experiences
- **Daily functioning**: "how things have been going recently" (past 30 days) — NOT enduring characteristics

This distinction helps the user understand that some patterns are more modifiable than others, and that current difficulties in functioning or regulation do NOT necessarily reflect fixed personality characteristics.

## Handling Construct Overlap

The assessment EXPLICITLY acknowledges and explains expected overlap between domains rather than treating it as measurement error.

### Required Overlap Explanations (use when relevant patterns appear)

**Personality traits ↔ Emotion regulation**:
"People higher in neuroticism often report more difficulty with emotion regulation, but these describe different aspects — neuroticism describes your typical emotional reactivity, while emotion regulation describes the strategies you use to manage emotions."

**Personality functioning ↔ Attachment**:
"These domains overlap because both involve how you experience yourself in relationships. Personality functioning describes the overall coherence of your self and interpersonal processes, while attachment patterns describe specific relationship tendencies around closeness and distance."

**Sleep ↔ Functioning**:
"Poor sleep often affects daily functioning, so lower scores in both areas may reflect this connection rather than separate problems."

**Neuroticism ↔ Everything**:
Higher neuroticism often correlates with lower scores in emotion regulation, sleep quality, and functioning. When you see this pattern, name it as a connected system rather than listing separate problems.

## Dimensional, Not Categorical

- Use dimensional language throughout: continuums and ranges, NOT categories or types
- "Psychopathology was no more likely to generate taxonic findings than normal variation" — dimensional models are scientifically preferred
- Never create categories like "you are an anxious person" — use "your responses suggest higher sensitivity to..."
- The DSM-5 itself acknowledges "a growing body of scientific evidence favors dimensional concepts"
